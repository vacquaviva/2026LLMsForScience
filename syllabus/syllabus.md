## Course Description

This course provides a structured, practice-based introduction to the emerging ecosystem of
generative AI tools—particularly those based on Large Language Models (LLMs)—and their
potential to enhance scientific workflows. We will use one or more LLM tools each week to carry
out a specific scientific task, compare the performance of different tools and compare their output
to human-generated work, and reflect on what was valuable or unhelpful. In the final project, each
student will design and evaluate a multi-step scientific workflow or agentic pipeline using LLMs.

**Disclaimer**: I (probably) know more about this stuff than your average faculty member, but I am
far from an expert on many of these topics! I see my role here as a facilitator and collector of
resources, but I can't "teach" you the material - we will be learning together. I think it's very
important that students have the physical and mental space to learn about AI tools and to develop
their own opinions and frameworks, and young people are the ones whose job description will be
mostly affected by AI, so it's only fair to give them (you!) a bigger voice. I am very curious to hear
your thoughts and experiences, and I hope we will get to the end of this course in a different place
from where we started. To compensate for my own ignorance, I know a ton of brilliant people
across communities, and I plan to invite many guest lecturers who will speak with us about
different topics and demo various tools.

## Course Goals

The primary goals are:
1. **To develop a working understanding** of the capabilities and limitations of current LLM-
based tools relevant to scientific research (e.g., coding assistants, literature-search agents,
data-analysis copilots, writing aids, RAG systems, and agentic workflows).

2. **To figure out what AI use means to you**, as a person and a scientist, and how (or if) you
can integrate AI tools in your research workflow without compromising scientific rigor,
reproducibility, creativity, critical judgment, and whatever else is important to you. I aim
to provide a judgement-free environment for these discussions.

3. **To reflect on the ethical and societal context of AI.** I will provide curated readings, but
I mostly hope to have healthy collective discussions on responsible AI use, AI safety, bias,
provenance of information, academic integrity, and the role of LLMs in higher education
and scientific research.

## Previous Knowledge Expected

Students enrolling in this course are expected to have:

• Experience with scientific computing (Python preferred). Knowing machine learning
fundamentals may help (honestly, I don't know how much) but is certainly not required.

• Active involvement in scientific research, preferably at Ph.D. level; the course will be
mostly useful if you can tailor the content to your ongoing research, and you will be
expected to bring your research problems into the classroom.

• Curiosity, independence, self-motivation, and a collaborative spirit, as the success of
the course relies on active experimentation, constructive critique of your own work and
that of others.

No prior experience with LLMs is required.

## Grading rubric

This class is participation based, so attendance and participation count for 80% of the grade, and
the final project will be 20%. I of course understand if you need to miss a class or two because
[life], but you are expected to be in class on a regular basis (talk to me if you need to miss more
than 4 classes) and to be an active practitioner. There will be, for example, assignments from week
to week (Wed to Mon); I won't give them a traditional grade, but "participating" means getting
them done timely and with care and attention so we can critique them together, as well as doing
the readings, participating in discussions, etc. Most likely, I will ask all of you to add a slide in a
Google document on a weekly basis, and then ask 2-3 people to sign up every week for a "deep
dive" into the week's assignment; you will be expected to sign up for this at least 2x/semester.
